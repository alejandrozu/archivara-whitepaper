Executive Summary

Two engines.

Archivara is not a single software tool. It is a dual-engine ecosystem engineered to accelerate the full lifecycle of scientific discovery. The first engine is a proprietary scientific archive: a structured, metadata-rich repository where AI-generated papers, models, code, and datasets live with full reproducibility information, citation graphs, and semantic search. The second engine is an advanced autonomous AI agent capable of running agentic research workflows, improving existing research, generating novel proofs, and shipping verifiable results across mathematics, physics, and computational science.

Together, these two engines form a virtuous loop: every paper added to the archive enriches the agent's context, every agent discovery strengthens the archive, and every verified result expands the platform's scientific credibility. Unlike static pre-print servers or isolated LLM wrappers, Archivara is the only platform where the archive and the agent compound each other's value, creating a technical and data moat that widens with every interaction.

The results in our archive have been produced by our agent and verified by the Archivara team. They represent genuine, peer-reviewable advances in open mathematical and scientific problems, demonstrating the platform's real-world capability to accelerate innovation, not merely to assist it.

Pillar I, Algorithmic Optimization

A Generalizable
Optimization Intelligence

The single most important capability of an AI agent built for science is the ability to find solutions to hard optimization problems that have resisted human effort for years, and to do so not once, but repeatedly, across entirely different mathematical domains. Archivara's agent has demonstrated this capability across three distinct, independently verified problems, establishing a pattern of generalized optimization intelligence that goes far beyond memorization or pattern-matching.

Problem 01 / 03

The 16x16 Matrix Breakthrough

Matrix multiplication is the computational primitive underpinning all of modern AI. Every transformer attention head and every neural network forward pass reduces to matrix multiplication. Archivara's agent discovered a fundamentally new algebraic structure that reduces the number of scalar multiplications required to multiply two 16x16 matrices, by exploiting a previously unknown tensor decomposition of the bilinear map. This structure does not follow from existing Strassen-style recursion or known rank decompositions. It maps directly onto GPU tensor core tile sizes for immediate hardware-level impact, and because it exposes a generalizable algebraic pattern, it is recursively applicable to larger matrix dimensions, making it a foundational result rather than an isolated trick.

The Abstract Reasoning Challenge

In November 2025, Fields Medalist Terence Tao, working with Google DeepMind on mathematical AI problems, posed the key open question: can AI systems that perform well in lower-level combinatorics achieve comparable results in domains requiring "forbidding levels of abstraction"? He identified unsolved problems as a "long-tail distribution" where AI, deployed at scale, could systematically contribute, but only if it were capable of genuine conceptual leaps and not merely exhaustive search. Archivara's Erdos 897 result is a direct answer to that challenge: an original chain of thought, independently constructed, demonstrating that Archivara's agent operates precisely at the abstract reasoning frontier Tao identified. For researchers, this means a co-investigator capable of tackling problems where existing literature offers no direct foothold. For enterprises, it means reasoning infrastructure that generalizes to any domain where expert human capacity is scarce.

Pillar IV, Physics Discovery

Beyond Mathematics:
Discovering Physical Laws

Archivara's most recent published paper marks a decisive departure from the landscape of AI math tools: the agent trained a physics model directly from observational trajectory data and extracted the underlying governing equations in closed symbolic form. This is not problem-solving within mathematics. This is empirical science, the machine equivalent of Kepler deriving his laws from Tycho Brahe's planetary tables.

Pillar IV, What Was Done

Training a Model on Nature

Archivara's agent trained a physics-informed model on dynamical trajectory data, the kind of observational data that is the raw material of all experimental science. Rather than being told the equations governing the system, the model learned the Hamiltonian structure of the system from position and momentum observations, then applied symbolic regression to the learned energy functionals to recover the governing equations in closed, human-readable form. The result: governing laws extracted autonomously from data, with no prior knowledge of what those laws should be.

Tools That Do the Work
Real Scientists Have to Do

Alongside the physics paper, Archivara's agent produced four papers in the domain of asteroid characterization, three focused tools and one compilation, each addressing a concrete, unsolved operational problem that practicing astronomers and planetary scientists face in their daily research. These are not demonstrations. They are working scientific software, published with full reproducible code.

Pillar V, Tool 01 / 04

Lightcurve Period Extraction

Every asteroid rotates. Measuring that rotation period from sparse, noisy ground-based photometry is one of the most common and most tedious tasks in small-body astronomy. Archivara's agent produced a full Python tool for automated lightcurve period extraction, handling phase folding, period disambiguation, and confidence estimation, a tool that saves an observing astronomer hours of manual fitting per target.

Tools Plus Archive Equals a New Kind of Scientific Infrastructure

Each of the four asteroid papers delivers a working, standalone tool. But the deeper significance is what happens when those tools are combined. A scientist who uploads new observational photometry can now invoke the period extraction tool, feed its output directly into the 3D shape reconstruction tool, combine the resulting shape model with the spectroscopic typing tool, and then run the full pipeline through the compilation workflow, end to end, with no new code written. This is not a suite of disconnected utilities. This is the beginning of a composable scientific software stack, one where each new tool deposited in the archive becomes a building block available to every future workflow. For research groups without dedicated software engineers, and for institutions where instrument time is expensive, this composability is transformative. Archivara's archive does not just store results. It accumulates capability.
